The Quiet Checklist Big Companies Use Before Approving a Supplier

Before we get into this month’s insight on supplier due diligence, keep this handy.

This is a simple checklist enterprise procurement and finance teams use to choose suppliers. They filter for professionalism, scale and experience, financial stability and strength.
 

Before Onboarding Any Supplier, Check the Following:

□ Has the supplier shown stable revenue over the last 3-5 years? Check revenue trends and volatility.
□ Are margins healthy enough to sustain operations? Check operating margins to spot stress pricing.
□ Is the supplier over-leveraged? Check borrowings and debt-equity ratio.
□ Do they rely heavily on short-term borrowings? Check borrowing mix for refinancing risk.
□ Are payables stretched or erratic? Check days payable and working capital cycle.
□ Any history of legal disputes or regulatory issues? Check litigations and compliance flags.
□ Are promoters or directors linked to past failures or defaults? Check director and group associations.
□ Does the supplier's scale match your order size? Check whether your volumes could strain their balance sheet.
□ Do you have backup suppliers mapped? Check concentration risk.

If any 3 boxes = unchecked → Tighten terms

If 5 boxes = unchecked → Don't onboard

How a Large Manufacturer Uses Tofler Before Finalising Vendors

A procurement head at a listed manufacturer shared a simple shift they made.

On paper, most suppliers looked fine. Yet every disruption traced back to one thing: financial stress they didn't spot early.

Now, they review the supplier's financial strength over time: revenue stability, profitability, borrowings, working capital, and visible risk signals.

Nothing fancy. No complex models. Just patterns.

This simple step has changed how they operate:

  • They avoid suppliers operating on financial edge
  • They catch stress before it shows up in delivery delays
  • They negotiate better payment terms
  • They reduce emergency sourcing
  • They protect production schedules
  • And most importantly, they sleep better during peak cycles
“Most supplier failures aren't surprises. They're patterns that were missed early.”

Quiet Point to Remember

Supplier risk is not just an operations issue.
It's a balance sheet issue.

One weak supplier can impact:

production timelines • inventory planning • customer commitments • working capital • brand credibility

You don't need more suppliers.
You need financially resilient suppliers.
